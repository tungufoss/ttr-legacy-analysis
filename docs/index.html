Ticket to Ride Legacy — Data Dive

One campaign, 12 plays, 5 companies — reconstructed from the dead letter office

What on earth is Ticket to Ride Legacy?

Ticket to Ride (TtR) is Alan R. Moon’s classic gateway board game: a set-collection and network-building train game. Most players where I come from are familiar with Europe or Nordic Countries, but regardless of which TtR map you know, the core gameplay remains largely the same: collect coloured train cards, claim routes between cities, complete destination tickets for points, and score additional bonuses from map-specific objectives. The player with the most points at the end of the game wins.

Ticket to Ride Legacy: Legends of the West (2023) gives that familiar formula the legacy treatment pioneered by Rob Daviau and Matt Leacock in Pandemic Legacy (Seasons 1, 2 and 0 — in that order). Across a twelve-game campaign, the outcome of each session permanently changes future games. The map expands frontier by frontier, new cards and mechanics are introduced, existing components are modified with stickers or punches, and — unlike many legacy games — almost nothing is destroyed. Instead, components eventually retire to the Dead Letter Office, creating a persistent record of the campaign as the story unfolds.

WarningSpoiler warning

This report is one long spoiler. It contains the complete campaign, including every frontier, the fully revealed map, destination tickets, postcards, hidden card values, campaign events, and final scoring. If you think you might play TtR Legacy: Legends of the West in the future, stop reading now and come back after twelve games.

Our campaign

Our campaign was played by five players. Throughout this report they are referred to by their player colours — Green, Black, Red, Yellow, and Blue. Since each player controls a railroad company throughout the campaign, the terms player and company are used interchangeably.

The game itself was a Christmas present from Green and Black to Black’s sisters, Red and Yellow, and Yellow’s husband, Blue. Everyone at the table was already well versed in TtR — particularly Europe — and are either budding hobbyists to hard-core gamers. Only Green and Black had previous experience with legacy games, having completed Pandemic Legacy Seasons 1 and 2 (with Season 0 still on the shelf of opportunity), and they also have some 20+ plays of Gloomhaven under their belt (still unfinished, but the dragon is nigh). As Pandemic Legacy and Gloomhaven are cooperative games in which players work together against the game, Legends of the West was everyone’s first competitive legacy campaign.

Although the campaign consists of only twelve games, ours spanned roughly two and a half years. The game was received as a Christmas present in 2023 and the first session took place in February 2024, but after several games the campaign paused from late 2024 until April 2026. In the meantime, life happened: pregnancies, the arrival of two new family members in 2025, and the usual challenge of finding time to get everyone back around the table.

After the campaign concluded, the complete game state was painstakingly reconstructed by hand. Every remaining card in the Storeroom, Post Office, Company Vaults, and the entire Dead Letter Office was catalogued card by card and entered into the database on which the analyses in this report are based.

Campaign basics

Legends of the West is played over twelve games spanning the years 1865 to 1898, with each session advancing the calendar by three years (i.e., 1865:3:1898). Every player controls a railroad company whose progress persists throughout the campaign. Between games, the map expands westward through newly opened Frontiers (jigsaw pieces that gradually form the board), while new cards, mechanics, and scoring opportunities themed to each frontier are introduced. As a result, every campaign unfolds differently, as the order in which these mechanics appear depends on the sequence in which the frontiers are explored.

Unlike a traditional game of TtR, companies develop asymmetrically over time. Employees provide unique abilities for a single game (my personal favourite is the Financier). The game is seeded with Big Cities (with more added during the campaign); every time a company builds a route into a Big City, it draws one bonus train card from the face-down deck (but only one bonus card per turn — even if a single route connects two Big Cities at once). Company Headquarters — permanent stickers placed on small city markers — create ongoing sources of income for their owners. The campaign’s recurring villain, Mama O’Connell, has a habit of collecting money that would otherwise be yours: she can establish headquarters of her own, and she frequently appears in the event deck demanding payment, while other events may reward companies that meet certain conditions. Components, primarily cards, are never destroyed. Instead, they may be modified and stored for future games until they are eventually retired to the Dead Letter Office — or, in some cases, simply depleted, such as a fully used sticker sheet.

Unlike traditional TtR, Legends of the West tracks dollars rather than victory points. Money is earned from completed destination tickets, coins remaining in hand, bonuses for trains left at the end of the game, favourable events, and the $2 toll other companies pay when building into one of your headquarters cities. Money is lost through failed destination tickets, unfavourable events, payments claimed by Mama O’Connell, and payments claimed by other companies (note you can also pay these tolls with train cards instead). Every game begins with each company holding $5 (although employee abilities may increase this starting amount).

NoteWhy 1865–1898?

The campaign follows the great American railroad boom. It begins in 1865, the year the American Civil War ended, and concludes in 1898 as the nineteenth century — and the era of westward railroad expansion — draws to a close. The rulebook deliberately leans into familiar themes of frontier expansion and the Wild West, while also acknowledging that this history came at a significant human cost through the dispossession of Native American peoples and the exploitation of railroad labourers. It is worth keeping this broader historical context in mind while enjoying the game’s lighter treatment of the period.

Score progression

When the campaign starts, the board is minimal and a game is relatively quick (less than an hour) — and it is hard to tell what a “good” score even looks like. What we noticed is that as the campaign progressed, the scores got higher. The more valuable tickets would be sought after and depleted: one feature of the legacy system that keeps the ticket deck from bloating is that whenever a company completes a ticket, it punches the card — some tickets allow one punch, others two (more on that later) — and once full, the ticket is retired to the Dead Letter Office.

The missing-player payout

One feature of the campaign deserves a confession. The rulebook is initially very vague about absent players: it merely says the campaign can continue even when not everyone is present. We took that to mean a missing player would simply receive nothing — hence the scheduling nightmare and the long hiatus. Only at the end of the campaign does a story deck card make up for those missed games, awarding a recommended dollar amount for each game a player sat out. Rather than attempting to recreate the absent player’s result, the designers simply award a fixed amount per game of the campaign — presumably chosen to approximate what a typical company would have earned during that game.

Because our campaign contains complete results for all five companies, we can evaluate how well these recommendations match observed play. Are they an unbiased estimate of an average player’s earnings? Do they systematically overestimate or underestimate actual performance? And would following the rule have unfairly advantaged or disadvantaged a player over the course of the campaign?

The remainder of this section compares the official “missing player” values against the observed campaign results — effectively an evaluation of one of the game’s hidden balancing mechanisms.

Interpretation

If the recommendations closely matched observed play, they would offer a fair way to handle missed sessions. The data says they are increasingly stingy: the guideline is roughly calibrated for the first half of the campaign, but from 1883 onward the table’s economy inflates (bigger tickets, headquarters tolls, richer events) while the guideline grows only linearly. Averaged over the whole campaign, four of the five companies out-earned the guideline by $13–19 per game; only Blue essentially broke even against it (average difference $-1.4). With just 12 paired observations per company, none of the Wilcoxon tests reach conventional significance (Green comes closest at p = 0.074), so the per-company differences should be read as descriptive. Still, the direction is consistent: skipping a late-campaign game and taking the official payout would have been a bad trade for everyone except perhaps Blue.

Opening the frontiers

How the map grows (per the official rules): at the end of a game, the winner chooses which jigsaw piece to add next — its cities and tickets come into play from the next game on. The dead letter office remembers the order: frontier cards sit between dated story markers in the pile, giving both the sequence and (with the one-frontier-per-game constraint) the year each section became playable. Drag the slider (or press play) to watch the board unfold from the initial East Coast start area; cities are colored by jigsaw section, and tracks appear once both their endpoints are unlocked.

Code 
frontier_map <- c(
  "great plains" = "Great Plains", "florida" = "Florida",
  "sierra madre" = "Sierra Madre", "haunted waste" = "Haunted Wastes",
  "badlands" = "Badlands", "california" = "California",
  "open range" = "Open Range", "cascadia" = "Cascadia"
)

unlocks <- dbGetQuery(con, "
  SELECT s.id, s.name, w.latest_year
  FROM story_cards s
  JOIN v_retirement_window w ON w.run_id = s.id
") |>
  mutate(key = tolower(name)) |>
  filter(key %in% names(frontier_map)) |>
  arrange(desc(id)) |>   # deeper in the pile = chosen earlier
  transmute(section = unname(frontier_map[key]), year = latest_year) |>
  # a frontier is chosen at the end of one game and playable from the
  # next, so in-play years must strictly increase (one per game); this
  # also corrects two markers that sit scrambled in the pile
  mutate(year = Reduce(
    function(prev, cur) max(cur, prev + 3),
    year, accumulate = TRUE
  ))

Hotspots

Which cities did completed tickets actually connect? Each punch on a ticket counts once for each of its two endpoint cities. Hover a city for its name, punch count, and most frequent visitor — the company that punched the most tickets through it.

Code 
city_players <- dbGetQuery(con, "
  WITH endpoint_punches AS (
    SELECT t.from_city AS city, p.color AS player, p.punches FROM tickets t
    JOIN ticket_punches p ON p.ticket_id = t.ticket_id
    UNION ALL
    SELECT t.to_city, p.color, p.punches FROM tickets t
    JOIN ticket_punches p ON p.ticket_id = t.ticket_id
  )
  SELECT city, player, SUM(punches) AS punches
  FROM endpoint_punches GROUP BY city, player
")

top_visitors <- city_players |>
  filter(punches > 0) |>
  group_by(city) |>
  filter(punches == max(punches)) |>
  summarise(
    top_label = paste0(paste(player, collapse = " & "),
                       " (", first(punches), ")"),
    .groups = "drop"
  )

hot <- city_players |>
  group_by(city) |>
  summarise(punches = sum(punches), .groups = "drop") |>
  left_join(top_visitors, by = "city") |>
  inner_join(coords, by = "city") |>
  mutate(tooltip = paste0(
    "<b>", city, "</b><br>punches: ", punches,
    "<br>most frequent visitor: ", coalesce(top_label, "none")
  ))

When was the network built?

Retired tickets sit between dated story markers in the dead letter office, which bounds when they were completed. Using the midpoint of each ticket’s retirement window as a rough timestamp:

Code 
era_tickets <- dbGetQuery(con, "
  SELECT t.from_city, t.to_city,
         (COALESCE(w.earliest_year, 1865) + COALESCE(w.latest_year, 1901)) / 2 AS mid_year,
         SUM(p.punches) AS punches
  FROM tickets t
  JOIN v_retirement_window w ON w.run_id = t.run_id
  JOIN ticket_punches p ON p.ticket_id = t.ticket_id
  WHERE t.active = 0
  GROUP BY t.ticket_id
  HAVING punches > 0
") |>
  mutate(era = cut(mid_year,
    breaks = c(1864, 1874, 1886, 1901),
    labels = c("early (1865-1874)", "mid (1877-1886)", "late (1889-1901)")
  )) |>
  inner_join(coords, by = c("from_city" = "city")) |>
  rename(lat_a = lat, lon_a = lon) |>
  inner_join(coords, by = c("to_city" = "city")) |>
  rename(lat_b = lat, lon_b = lon)
